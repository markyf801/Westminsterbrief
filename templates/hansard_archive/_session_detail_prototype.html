Student Loans (Repayment)

Commons Oral Questions 5 March 2025 View on Hansard ↗
Higher Education Student Finance Repayment Thresholds Graduate Outcomes Plan 2 Loans
↓ Download transcript (Word) 47 contributions
#
Josh MacAlister Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State · Lab
The repayment threshold for Plan 2 loans is currently set at £25,000. The Government keeps all aspects of student finance under review, and our priority is ensuring that the system is fair for both graduates and the taxpayer. We are committed to building a higher education system that delivers real value — for students, for employers, and for the wider economy. We will continue to work with the sector to ensure that graduates are not burdened with unsustainable debt.
#
Helen Whately Con
The Minister talks about fairness, but can he explain to graduates who took out loans under Plan 2 why they are paying back at 9% above £25,000 — a threshold that was frozen for years — while the interest rate continues to compound? Many of my constituents are seeing their loan balance rise despite making consistent payments. That is not what they were promised when they signed up.
#
Josh MacAlister Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State · Lab
I understand the hon. Member's concern, and it is one that many graduates share. The previous Government froze the repayment threshold for several years, which did increase the burden on lower and middle earners. This Government inherited that situation. We are actively examining the threshold structure as part of our broader review of student finance sustainability. I am not in a position to make announcements today, but I can assure the House that fairness for graduates is central to our approach. I would also point the hon. Member to the write-off provisions at 30 years — a significant proportion of Plan 2 borrowers will not repay in full, which is an intentional feature of the system's design.
#
Munira Wilson LD
The Minister references a "review" — but graduates cannot wait indefinitely for a review. Would he commit today to uprating the repayment threshold at least in line with earnings, so that the real value of the threshold does not continue to erode?
#
Carol Monaghan SNP
In Scotland, we do not have tuition fees. Students in England are taking on tens of thousands of pounds of debt for degrees. Could the Minister tell the House what assessment the Government have made of the chilling effect this debt burden has on graduates' decisions to buy homes, start families, or set up businesses?
#
Sir Edward Leigh Con
Given the extraordinary cost of the student loan book to the public finances — written off sums that run into the tens of billions — would the Minister consider whether a graduate tax model, as proposed by several economists, would better align incentives and remove the fiction that most graduates will ever repay in full?
#
Carla Denyer Green
Does the Minister accept that the current system functions less like a loan and more like a graduate tax in all but name — but with none of the transparency, fairness, or public understanding that an explicit graduate tax would carry? If so, why not make it one?
#
Lee Anderson Reform
The real problem is too many people going to university in the first place. Would the Minister consider redirecting student loan funding toward apprenticeships and vocational training, which deliver better outcomes for working people than many of the degrees currently being subsidised?
#
Lord Willetts CB · Crossbench
My Lords, as the minister who introduced the current system, I would urge caution about wholesale reform. The income-contingent model was designed specifically to protect graduates during periods of low earnings — that insurance function is its greatest virtue, and it would be lost in a move to a conventional graduate tax.
#
Lady Hermon Ind
Will the Minister assure the House that any changes to the repayment system will apply equally to students from Northern Ireland who hold Plan 2 loans and study at English universities?
