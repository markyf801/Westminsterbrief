## Stock-phrase drift

Civil Service writing uses stock phrases that encode meaning beyond their surface text. The significance is often in the sequence across multiple statements, not any single statement read in isolation.

### Commitment to timeline

| Phrase | Signal |
|---|---|
| "We will publish shortly" | High confidence, near-term |
| "We will publish in due course" | Timeline has slipped; internal blocker likely |
| "We are working to publish" | Active but uncertain |
| "We remain committed to publishing" | Defensive; external pressure building |
| "We will set out our plans in due course" | Decision not yet made |

**Archetype — post-16 skills white paper:** Government statements drifted "publishing shortly" → "publishing in due course" → "publishing shortly" again. Each statement looks fine on its own; the sequence reveals an internal blocker emerged and then resolved. This is the canonical example of why drift matters more than any single statement.

### Degree of seriousness / intent

| Phrase | Signal |
|---|---|
| "Considering" | Early stage, no commitment |
| "Actively considering" | Has moved up the priority list |
| "Keeping under review" | Not a priority; may be parked indefinitely |
| "We will" | Committed |
| "We intend to" | Committed but contingent |
| "The Government will consider" | Non-committal; process language |
| "We are exploring options" | No decision; may never progress |

### Degree of agreement with external input

| Phrase | Signal |
|---|---|
| "Welcome the contribution" | Taking it seriously |
| "Note the contribution" | Acknowledged, not endorsed |
| "Have taken careful note of" | Politely disagreeing |
| "Will consider the recommendations carefully" | Not yet decided; often precedes partial acceptance |
| "Does not accept the recommendation" | Rejected, but diplomatically |

### Parliamentary scrutiny signals

| Pattern | Signal |
|---|---|
| PQ answered with "I refer the Honourable Member to my answer of [date]" | Frustration with repeat probing; worth noting who is asking what repeatedly and at what frequency |
| Written ministerial statement issued on a Friday or recess eve | Attempting to minimise attention |
| APPG report receives a written ministerial statement reply | Being taken seriously |
| APPG report receives a press release reply | Being managed, not engaged with |
| Consultation outcome doesn't address particular submitter concerns | Those concerns were noted and rejected without explicit acknowledgement |
| Position gets vague rather than changing ("keeping options under review") | Quiet retreat without formal reversal |

## Manifesto pledge signals

- Pledges that disappear from departmental business plans and single departmental plans are usually about to be quietly dropped — track their presence/absence across successive plans, not just the manifesto text
- Pledges reframed as "ambitions" rather than "commitments" in subsequent documents signal weakening
- Pledges that acquire caveats ("subject to fiscal conditions", "when resources allow") in ministerial speeches are being conditionalised

---

## Committee engagement signals

- Oral evidence sessions where the minister brings an unusually large official team signal the department is worried about the scrutiny
- Committee reports that get a government response within 30 days (required minimum) but with very short responses signal the department wanted to close the loop quickly rather than engage substantively
- "The Government agrees in principle" in a committee response almost always means "the Government will not act on this"
- Select committee chairs asking questions on the floor of the House (oral questions, UQs) on a topic covered in a recent committee report signals the committee response was unsatisfactory

---

## How to use this in position-evolution analysis

When analysing a sequence of government statements on a topic:

1. Extract the specific phrases used across each statement
2. Map them to the signal register above
3. Identify drift: has the language become more or less committed over time?
4. Separate minister changes from position changes — a new minister inheriting "we are considering" language is not the same as a position change
5. Flag sequences that look neutral on their face but show drift when read together (the post-16 skills white paper pattern)
6. Be honest about continuity: if the language is consistent, say so. Don't manufacture a narrative of drift where the source shows stability
